# How to Find an Attorney Who Offers Free Consultations

Free initial consultations are the norm in most practice areas — personal injury, employment, family law, and criminal defense attorneys routinely offer them. But knowing that free consultations exist doesn't help you find the *right* attorney to consult.

Here's the practical guide.

## Why Most Attorneys Offer Free Consultations

The free consultation isn't charity. It's a sales call for both sides. The attorney wants to evaluate whether your case is worth taking. You want to evaluate whether this attorney is worth hiring.

For contingency-fee practices (personal injury, workers' comp), attorneys only get paid if you win, so they're selective. For hourly-rate practices, the consultation gives you a chance to assess fit before paying.

## Step 1: Match Practice Area to Your Case

Before you contact anyone, be specific about what kind of attorney you need:

- **Personal injury** — car accidents, slip and fall, medical malpractice
- **Employment** — wrongful termination, discrimination, wage theft
- **Family law** — divorce, custody, adoption
- **Criminal defense** — charges, DUI, expungement
- **Real estate** — landlord-tenant, closing disputes, title issues
- **Business** — contracts, IP, formation, disputes
- **Immigration** — visas, green cards, removal defense

Searching for "attorney" without a practice area gets you generalists who may have handled your case type once.

## Step 2: Narrow by Jurisdiction

An attorney licensed in California can't practice in New York federal court without being admitted to that bar. Make sure any attorney you consult is licensed and active in your state — and for federal cases, admitted to the relevant federal district.

This sounds obvious, but many national directories show out-of-state attorneys prominently.

## Step 3: Look at Actual Case History, Not Reviews

Reviews are gamed. Ratings are bought. The most reliable signal is whether an attorney has actually filed cases like yours in relevant courts.

For federal matters, you can look up docket history on PACER (federal case records) or through free tools like CourtListener. An attorney with 50 employment discrimination cases in the Southern District of New York is a meaningfully different match than one with 2.

## Step 4: Prepare Before the Consultation

Don't go in cold. A good consultation covers:

1. **The facts** — what happened, when, who was involved
2. **The documents** — contracts, emails, medical records, police reports
3. **Your goals** — settlement, full trial, injunction, criminal defense
4. **Your timeline** — statute of limitations issues
5. **Your budget** — hourly vs. contingency, what you can afford

Attorneys remember clients who come prepared. It signals you're serious.

## Step 5: What to Ask During the Consultation

- Have you handled cases like mine in this court?
- What's your assessment of the strengths and weaknesses?
- What's the likely timeline?
- How do you bill, and what's the engagement structure?
- Who in your office will actually work on my case?

If an attorney can't give you a direct answer on case history or timeline, that's a red flag.
