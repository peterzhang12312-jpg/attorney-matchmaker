# Federal Court vs. State Court: Which One Handles Your Case?

One of the most common mistakes people make when pursuing legal claims is not knowing whether their case belongs in federal or state court. Filing in the wrong venue can get your case dismissed — or force you to start over after the statute of limitations has run.

Here's the practical breakdown.

## The Two Court Systems

The United States has parallel court systems:

**State courts** handle the vast majority of cases: most criminal matters, family law, probate, personal injury, contract disputes, landlord-tenant, and state regulatory issues. Each state has its own system — trial courts, appellate courts, and a state supreme court.

**Federal courts** handle a narrower but important set of cases, defined by the U.S. Constitution and federal statutes.

## When Federal Court Has Jurisdiction

Federal courts can hear your case in three main situations:

### 1. Federal Question Jurisdiction

Your case involves a federal law, treaty, or constitutional provision. Examples:

- Employment discrimination under Title VII, ADA, or ADEA
- Civil rights violations under 42 U.S.C. § 1983
- Securities fraud under federal securities laws
- Bankruptcy (always federal)
- Patent, copyright, or trademark disputes
- Immigration matters
- Social Security disability appeals
- ERISA benefit claims (most employer-sponsored benefit plans)

If your claim arises under federal law, it can typically be filed in federal district court — and often *must* be.

### 2. Diversity Jurisdiction

Even if no federal law is involved, federal courts can hear cases where:

- The parties are citizens of **different states** (or one is a foreign national), AND
- The **amount in controversy exceeds $75,000**

This is called "diversity jurisdiction." It exists because Congress worried that state courts might be biased in favor of in-state parties. Note: both conditions must be met. A $50,000 dispute between a New Yorker and a Californian stays in state court.

### 3. The United States Is a Party

If you're suing the federal government (or a federal agency), or the government is prosecuting you, the case is in federal court.

## When State Court Is the Right Forum

State court handles:

- **Criminal charges** under state law (the large majority of criminal cases)
- **Family law**: divorce, custody, child support, adoption
- **Probate and estate**: wills, guardianship
- **Real estate disputes**: property disputes, landlord-tenant, zoning
- **Most contract disputes**: business contracts, service agreements
- **Personal injury under state tort law**: car accidents, slip-and-fall (absent diversity)
- **Medical malpractice**
- **State regulatory violations**

## Concurrent Jurisdiction: Cases That Can Go Either Way

Some cases can be filed in either federal or state court. Employment discrimination claims are a common example — you can file EEOC charges and then pursue in federal court, or in some states, pursue parallel state claims in state court under equivalent state laws (like the NYSHRL in New York or FEHA in California).

State courts often have stronger plaintiff-side protections and broader remedies in discrimination cases. This is a strategic decision attorneys make based on local precedent.

## Why This Matters for Attorney Selection

An attorney who primarily practices in California state court has a different skill set from one who regularly files in the Northern or Southern District of California federal courts. Federal practice requires federal bar admission, familiarity with the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, and experience with federal judges.
